Methodology

The model doesn't care
about the narrative.

Five independent models. Live grading. No gut feelings, no hot takes, no bias toward name brands. Here's how it actually works — enough to understand it, not enough to copy it.

01

Vegas is very, very good at their job.

The sportsbook doesn't set a line to predict the outcome. It sets a line to split the public 50/50 so they collect the vig on both sides. That means public money, media narratives, and fan bias all move lines away from their true probability.

That's the inefficiency we're looking for. When the market misprices a game, we want to find it before it corrects. Not because we have insider information — because we're measuring things the public doesn't weight correctly.

The only question that matters
Not "who wins?" — Vegas already knows that. The question is: where does the market reliably get the number wrong? Years of studying college football lines tell us which specific game patterns the public consistently misprices. Those are the games we hunt — not every line that looks off, just the ones that fit a pattern we've already mapped.
02

Five reads. One question.

Most public models try to be the one right answer. We don't. We run five different models on every game — each measuring something different, each with its own blind spots — and we look at how they interact.

SP+ measures efficiency. Elo measures head-to-head record. PPA measures expected points per play. Advanced stats measure the things the box score misses. Pace+, our in-house model, projects how much time each team will spend with the ball and what they can do with it.

Each model answers the same question — what does the data say this game should look like — and they answer it differently. We don't look for agreement. We look at how each model reads the game in the context of the other four. The disagreement is part of the signal.

03

The market mispricing comes first.

Picks don't start with the models. They start with the market.

We've spent years studying where the market consistently misprices certain types of college football games. Specific kinds of matchups, specific tempo profiles, specific spread or total ranges where the public's intuition reliably points one way and the data reliably points the other. Those are the situations we hunt.

Once we've found a game in one of those situations, we look at how the five models are reading it. The models aren't searching for picks — they're confirming whether the mispricing we expect is actually there. The market gap is the why. The model read is the how.

A pick is published only when the situation, the line, and the model read all line up.

04

What the model ignores.

Discipline is as important as methodology. There are things that sound like they should matter that the data consistently shows don't — at least not in the way the public thinks.

✕
Injuries announced after our pick is set. We pick off opening lines. We don't chase movement caused by news we didn't have when the pick was made.
✕
Revenge games, rivalry narratives, must-win situations. These stories are already priced in by the public. We're looking for mispriced talent gaps, not motivation angles.
✕
Recency bias. A team that scored 52 points last week isn't necessarily a better bet this week. The model weighs rolling performance but doesn't overreact to a single outlier.
✕
Brand names. Alabama, Georgia, Ohio State — the public always overvalues them. The models don't know or care what the ESPN narrative is.
