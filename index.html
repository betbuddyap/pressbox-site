The Model

Five reads. One discipline.

Every game on the board runs through five independent models, each measuring something different. None of them tell the whole story alone. That's the point.

SP+ · The efficiency baseline.

Bill Connelly's tempo- and opponent-adjusted measure of college football efficiency. Forward-looking, not résumé-driven — it weighs how a team plays, not whether they've already won. If you're lucky in a win, SP+ doesn't give you full credit. If you're unlucky in a loss, it keeps believing in you.

Elo · The head-to-head record.

A rating that updates after every game based on who beat whom and by how much. Borrowed from chess and adapted for football. Slower to react than the efficiency models, faster to react than the polls. Tells us who's been winning against the schedule they've actually played.

PPA · Points-per-play.

Predicted Points Added on a play-by-play basis. Strips away score and clock context to ask: what is each snap actually worth? A team can dominate PPA and still lose the game. We care about the dominating, not the losing.

Advanced · The deeper efficiency layer.

Success rate, explosiveness, havoc, and field-position stats — the kind of numbers that separate teams who win because of how they play from teams who win because of luck. Where the box score lies, advanced stats usually catch it.

Pace+ · In-house. The possession model.

Built for college football's biggest variable — pace. Pace+ projects how much time each team will spend with the ball, then uses weighted statistical inputs to decide what each team can do in the time they get. Possession decides the ceiling. Pace+ figures out how close each team gets to it. It's the backbone of our spread picks.
